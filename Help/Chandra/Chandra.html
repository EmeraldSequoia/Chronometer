Chandra

Chandra (Hindi for "Moon") shows the near side of the Moon as seen from the watch's current location and time. The faint lines on the dark side are the edges of the covers that move to define the position of the terminator. The image rotates to correspond to the Moon's orientation in the sky relative to the local horizon.

There are also two tiny "stars", a red one near the Moon's limb and a blue one near the edge of the case. They indicate the Moon's altitude (elevation above the horizon) and azimuth respectively. In this example, the Moon is about to set just south of west.

In Set mode, the upper pusher advances to the next even quarter phase; the lower pusher advances 15 minutes. As usual, you can tap the day and month windows to advance a day or a (calendar) month.

On the back, the hand indicates the Moon's angular separation from the Sun (technically, it is the difference between the Moon's ecliptic longitude and the Sun's; it ignores the difference in declination). This is similar but not exactly the same as its "age" in the current lunar cycle. (Geneva has a more accurate age indicator.) Straight up is new moon (0 age), at right is first quarter (about 7.5 days old), straight down is full moon (about 15 days), left is 3rd (last) quarter (about 22 days old). A complete lunar cycle is about 29.5 days (though it actually varies by several hours).

Finding a very "young" (thin crescent) Moon can be very challenging. There are many complicating factors; the phase angle shown here is just the beginning.

The numbers in the quarter icons show the date when that phase occurred or will occur. The two clockwise from the hand are in the future, and the two behind the hand show dates in the past. Frequently the dates are in different calendar months (in this example the current date is June 2, the Moon is about 123 degrees from the Sun, the previous 1st quarter was on May 30 and the next full moon will be June 7).

In Set mode, tapping one of the phase icons advances to the exact time of that event; tapping again advances to the next (lunar) month. You can also drag the hand or tap the date windows.

The background is a NASA image of the lunar far side.
